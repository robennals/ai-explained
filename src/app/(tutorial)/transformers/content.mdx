## Add Residual

We want to stack many transformer blocks — GPT-3 stacks 96 of them. But there's a problem: every time information passes through a block, it gets changed. After dozens of blocks, the original meaning can get completely lost. It's like a game of telephone — each person changes the message a little, and by the end it's nonsense.

The fix is a **residual connection** (the red paths in the diagram that skip around each step). Instead of *replacing* the input with the output, you *add* the output to the input:

**output = input + what the layer computed**

This way, the original information always makes it through untouched. Each block only needs to figure out a small improvement to add on top. It's like a highway bypass around a small town — even if the detour through town is confusing, the highway carries the original signal through safely.

<ResidualConnectionWidget>
Watch what happens to a signal as it passes through many layers. Without residual connections, it turns to mush. With them, the original stays clear and each layer adds useful improvements. Try cranking the layers up to 20 or more.
</ResidualConnectionWidget>

<Callout type="info" title="A team of editors">
Imagine editing a document. Without residual connections, each editor rewrites the entire thing from scratch — after 96 rewrites, nothing remains of the original. With residual connections, each editor just adds notes and corrections to the same document. The original is always there, getting better with each pass.
</Callout>

## Layer Normalization

After each residual add step, the numbers in our vectors can start to drift. Some might grow huge (in the thousands), while others shrink close to zero. This makes it really hard for the next layer to do its job — the numbers are all over the place.

**Layer normalization** fixes this by rescaling the numbers after each step. It shifts them so their average is 0, and stretches or squishes them so their spread is about 1. Think of it like an automatic volume knob on a microphone — no matter how loud or quiet the speaker is, the output always comes out at a comfortable level.

Try pushing values to extremes and watch layer normalization snap them back to a reasonable range. Then see what happens when you stack layers without it — the numbers quickly explode or collapse.
</LayerNormWidget>

## Feed-Forward Network

The second half of each transformer block is a **feed-forward network** — a small neural network with two layers and an activation function in between (called GELU, which works a lot like the ReLU you saw in [Chapter 3](/neurons)).

Here's the key difference between attention and the feed-forward network: attention lets words *talk to each other* and share information, while the feed-forward network lets each word *think about* the information it has gathered. It processes each word's vector independently — word 1's feed-forward computation doesn't know anything about word 5's.

What does this "thinking" actually do? Researchers have found that these layers work like a kind of **memory**. During training, the feed-forward weights learn to store facts and associations — things like "the capital of France is Paris" or "after 'once upon a' the next word is usually 'time'." The attention layers figure out *which* facts are relevant; the feed-forward layers *recall* them.

The network first expands each word's vector to 4× its original size (giving it more room to work with), applies the activation function, then shrinks it back down. It's like spreading out your notes on a big desk to think things through, then packing them back into a neat folder.

## A Transformer That Tells Stories

Now let's try one that's big enough to be genuinely interesting. This transformer has about 2 million parameters — still tiny compared to ChatGPT (which is a million times bigger), but enough to write text that sometimes surprises you.

<LiveTransformerWidget>
Type a prompt and hit Generate to watch the transformer write, one word at a time. Try the temperature slider: low = safe and predictable, high = creative and weird. Click any word it generates to see what other words it was considering.
</LiveTransformerWidget>

This is **auto-regressive generation** — the trick every language model uses:

1. Look at the text so far
2. Predict the most likely next word
3. Add that word to the text
4. Go back to step 1

That's it. That's how ChatGPT writes. That's how Claude writes. The model you just played with does exactly the same thing — just smaller.

**Temperature** controls how adventurous the model is. At temperature 0, it always picks the single most likely word — safe, but boring and repetitive. Turn it up and the model starts taking risks — more creative, but eventually nonsensical.

<KeyInsight>
This model was trained to do one thing: predict the next word. Nobody told it what grammar is. Nobody explained how stories work. Nobody taught it that characters have names. It figured all of this out on its own, just by trying to guess what comes next. Scale this up — more parameters, more training data — and you get the AI systems people use every day.
</KeyInsight>

Different attention heads learn completely different jobs:
- Some focus on **nearby words** — they're figuring out local grammar
- Some focus on **the beginning of the sentence** — they're tracking the overall topic
- Some focus on **specific patterns** — like connecting pronouns back to the nouns they refer to

Nobody programmed these specializations. The model figured out on its own that having different heads do different jobs leads to better predictions.

## A Nuance: Prompt vs Generation

Earlier, we said each word can only look backward. But there's a subtlety.

When you type a prompt like "Once upon a time," all those words are already known — you typed them in. There's no reason "upon" can't look at "time," since they're both sitting right there. The no-peeking rule is really only needed for the words the model is *generating*, because those don't exist yet.

Some models take advantage of this: they let all the prompt words look at each other freely, and only apply the causal mask to the generated words. The attention pattern changes from a pure triangle to a filled square (the prompt) with a triangle extending below it (the generation).

## From Tiny to Titan

You've now played with transformers ranging from 50,000 to 2 million parameters. The architecture is the same across all of them — tokenize, embed, attend, think, predict. The only difference is scale.

GPT-4 has over a trillion parameters. Claude is similarly large. Same architecture, same math, same word-by-word generation loop. Just more layers, wider embeddings, bigger feed-forward networks, more attention heads, trained on enormously more data.

And as you scale up, something surprising keeps happening: new abilities *emerge*. Arithmetic, reasoning, writing code, composing poetry, understanding humor — abilities that were never explicitly programmed, never directly trained for. They just appeared, as a side effect of getting better at predicting the next word. We'll explore why this happens in a later chapter.

## The Big Picture

Let's trace the path we've taken through these chapters:

1. **Everything is numbers** ([Chapter 1](/computation)) — text, images, and sound are all just lists of numbers
2. **Small improvements add up** ([Chapter 2](/optimization)) — gradient descent finds good solutions step by step
3. **Neural networks learn patterns** ([Chapter 3](/neurons)) — layers of neurons can learn to recognize anything
4. **Vectors capture meaning** ([Chapter 4](/vectors)) — similar things end up with similar numbers
5. **Embeddings give words geometry** ([Chapter 5](/embeddings)) — words live in a space where closeness = similarity
6. **Prediction requires understanding** ([Chapter 6](/next-word-prediction)) — to guess the next word, you need to *get* the sentence
7. **Attention lets words talk** ([Chapter 7](/attention)) — each word chooses which other words to listen to
8. **Position encoding adds order** ([Chapter 8](/positions)) — so "dog bit man" isn't the same as "man bit dog"
9. **The transformer wires it all together** (this chapter) — and meaning emerges from prediction

That's the architecture behind the AI revolution. Attention and feed-forward layers, with residual connections to keep information flowing and layer normalization to keep numbers stable, all trained to predict the next word. Simple pieces, carefully wired together, trained at enormous scale.
