# The Transformer

<Lead>
The transformer is the architecture behind every major AI you've heard of: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama. It's the wiring that turns the pieces from earlier chapters (embeddings, attention, positional encoding) into something that can read a sentence and tell you what comes next.

## A Transformer At a Glance

The core idea is simple. We pass a passage of text through a sequence of layers. Each layer creates a better representation of each token by combining what's already known about that token with information from other tokens. Attention is what picks out which other tokens have the relevant information.

The original paper that introduced transformers was called *"Attention Is All You Need"*, and the title was apt: attention is where most of the work happens.

The picture below shows a very simple transformer at work on a tiny sentence. The final layer's embedding of the last token contains enough information to predict the next word.

The representation of each token gets richer at each layer, as it pulls in information from the tokens it paid attention to.

This looks a lot like a regular [neural network](/neurons): boxes in layers, connected by links whose weights control how strongly the signal flows. The difference is that in a normal neural network the wiring is fixed, learned once during training. Here, attention decides which information flows where, sentence by sentence. And as you'll see later, each node has its own small neural network combining the incoming information with what it already knows.

## Large Language Models are Very Large

The playground above has 5 layers with one [attention head](/attention) per layer. Real transformers are much, much bigger. Most frontier models don't disclose their exact dimensions, but GPT-3 had 96 layers with 96 heads per layer, and the latest models are thought to be significantly larger.

Modern LLMs also use [embeddings](/embeddings) with thousands of [dimensions](/vectors) (12,288 for GPT-3). That gives enough room to encode something as specific as "a blue thing that belongs to a human astronaut, seen by that astronaut in the sky of Mars, which is a planet in Earth's solar system".

## Interpretability

The examples above have their representations written by hand rather than generated by a real transformer. One of the strange things about real transformers is that we usually don't know what the intermediate representations of tokens mean, or what a particular attention head or layer is trying to do. When you train a transformer, it isn't trying to produce layers, heads, or representations that make sense to humans. It's only trying to find representations that best help it complete its task, such as predicting the next word.

Working out the inner thought processes of transformers is an area of active research.

## The Big Picture

A quick recap of the path so far:

1. Everything is numbers ([Chapter 1](/computation)). Text, images, sound, all just lists of numbers.
2. Small improvements add up ([Chapter 2](/optimization)). Gradient descent finds good solutions step by step.
3. Neural networks learn patterns ([Chapter 3](/neurons)). Layers of neurons can learn anything.
4. Vectors capture meaning ([Chapter 4](/vectors)). Similar things end up with similar numbers.
5. Embeddings give words geometry ([Chapter 5](/embeddings)). Words live in a space where closeness equals similarity.
6. Prediction requires understanding ([Chapter 6](/next-word-prediction)). To guess the next word, you have to *get* the sentence.
7. Attention lets words talk ([Chapter 7](/attention)). Each word chooses which others to listen to.
8. Position encoding adds order ([Chapter 8](/positions)). So "dog bit man" isn't the same as "man bit dog".
9. The transformer wires it all together (this chapter). Meaning emerges from prediction.

The same architecture, scaled up trillions of times, is what powers ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.
