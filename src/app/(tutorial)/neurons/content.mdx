## The Building Block

Every neuron takes numbers in and produces a number out. It receives inputs from the previous layer, does a little computation, and passes a result to the next layer. So what happens inside one?

A neuron does two things:

1. *Weighted sum.* Each input gets multiplied by a *weight*, a number that controls how much that input matters. All the weighted inputs get added together, plus a constant called the *bias*. So far this is just multiplication and addition.
2. *Activation function.* The result is passed through a function that decides how much to pass on to the next layer. This is the *activation function*, and it's what makes a neuron more than arithmetic. Later in this chapter you'll see why it matters.

That's the whole thing. A neuron is a weighted sum followed by an activation function.

<NeuronDiagramWidget />

A neuron is multiplication, addition, and a function applied to the result. Wire millions of them together and you have GPT, or the human brain. The hard part isn't building the network. It's finding the right values for all those weights and biases. That's what training does.

## The Sigmoid

The activation function used here is the *sigmoid*, an S-shaped curve that smoothly squashes any number into the range 0 to 1. Large positive inputs come out close to 1. Large negative inputs come out close to 0. Everything in between gets a smooth, graded value. Think of it as a dimmer switch: instead of snapping between off and on like a light switch, it slides between them.

<SigmoidExplorerWidget>
Drag the input slider left and right. The output stays glued near 0 or 1 for extreme values, but changes rapidly in the middle. Where does the steepest part of the curve fall?
</SigmoidExplorerWidget>

Nature loves S-curves. A lot of real-world phenomena follow this shape: population growth, technology adoption, learning curves, the spread of diseases. If a trend doesn't look S-shaped, you're probably only seeing part of one. If it looks exponential, you're seeing the beginning. If it looks linear, you're seeing the middle. If it looks like diminishing returns, you're seeing the end.

<SigmoidZoomWidget />

The key property for neural networks is this: when you change a weight by a small amount, the output changes by a small amount. No sudden jumps, no unpredictable flips. That's what Chapter 2 said we need for optimization to work.

You can make the sigmoid *sharper* or *smoother* by scaling the weights. Multiply all the weights and bias by a large number and the S-curve steepens toward a hard step. Scale them down and it flattens into a gentle slope. The boundary stays in the same place. Only the sharpness changes.

<SharpnessExplorerWidget>
Drag the sharpness slider all the way up. The curve snaps almost like a light switch: 0 or 1, nothing in between. Now drag it down. The curve becomes a gentle slope.
</SharpnessExplorerWidget>

There's a catch. Very sharp neurons are harder to train. Optimization (from Chapter 2) works by measuring how a small change in a weight affects the output. On the flat parts of a sharp sigmoid, the output barely changes at all, so the slope is nearly zero. The optimizer gets stuck because it can't tell which direction to move. Real networks use various tricks to keep neurons in the smooth, trainable middle of the curve, but the basic point is enough for now: smoother neurons are easier to train.

## Logic Gates

You may know the basic logical operations: AND, OR, and NOT. They combine true-or-false values.

- *AND*: "Are both things true?" You need an umbrella if it's raining AND you're going outside.
- *OR*: "Is at least one thing true?" You'll get wet if it's raining OR someone sprays you with a hose.
- *NOT*: Flips the answer. NOT true is false. NOT false is true.

These operations are the basis for every digital computer. Inside every chip is a network of *logic gates*, tiny components that compute AND, OR, NOT, and similar operations on electrical signals.

</LogicGatePlaygroundWidget>

Wire enough gates together and you can compute anything. Real computer chips have billions of them. Your phone, your laptop, every digital device works this way.

<GateCircuitDiagramWidget />

But logic gates have a problem. Their outputs snap between 0 and 1 with nothing in between. There's no smooth gradient to follow, which means you can't train a network of logic gates using the optimization techniques from Chapter 2. You can't ask "how should I adjust this gate to make the answer a little more correct?" It's all or nothing.

## Neurons as Smooth Logic

A neuron is a smooth version of a logic gate. With the right weights, a single two-input neuron can compute AND, OR, NOT, and more. Unlike a logic gate, its output changes smoothly when you adjust the weights. That smoothness is what makes neurons trainable.

<NeuronPlaygroundWidget>
Click the AND, OR, NOT, and NAND buttons and the weights shift to compute each gate. Then try sliding the weights yourself. You can blend smoothly between different logic operations.
</NeuronPlaygroundWidget>

There's something else going on here beyond mimicking logic gates. When you slide an input to a value *between* 0 and 1, say 0.6, the output isn't forced to snap to "true" or "false." It can land anywhere: 0.73, 0.4, whatever. The neuron handles *degrees* naturally. "Probably true" rather than just "true" or "false."

This matters because real-world data is rarely cleanly true or false. Is this email spam? Probably. Is that shadow in the photo a cat? Maybe. A neuron can take uncertain inputs and produce a graded output.

<KeyInsight>
A neuron is a smooth, trainable logic gate. With the right weights it can compute AND, OR, NOT, and more, and because small changes to the weights produce small changes in the output, those weights can be found using optimization. Neurons also handle in-between values naturally, which makes them building blocks for computation over uncertain, continuous data.
</KeyInsight>

## Three Neurons Solve XOR

One more operation is worth knowing: *XOR*, short for "exclusive or." It means "one or the other, but not both." A single neuron can't solve it. No matter what weights you try, you can't get all four input combinations right at once.

But two neurons feeding into a third can. The two middle neurons are called *hidden neurons*. They don't produce the final answer. They compute intermediate results that the output neuron needs.

The trick:
- *Hidden Neuron 1* computes OR: "is at least one input on?"
- *Hidden Neuron 2* computes NAND (not-and): "are they not both on?"
- *Output neuron* computes AND of those two results: "at least one is on, AND they're not both on."

That's XOR. Three neurons, two layers, problem solved.

<Callout type="info" title="The XOR Crisis">
In 1969, Minsky and Papert published *Perceptrons*, proving that single-layer networks can't solve XOR. This was taken as proof that neural networks were a dead end, and it killed most neural network research for over a decade: the "AI winter." The fix was always sitting there, obvious in hindsight. Use more than one layer.
</Callout>

## What Hidden Layers Compute

To solve hard problems, you often need to compute intermediate things first. You can't jump straight from raw pixels to "that's a cat." You need stepping stones.

That's what hidden layers do. Each layer takes the previous layer's outputs and combines them into something more complex. The first hidden layer might detect simple patterns: edges, color patches, textures. The next layer combines those into parts: eyes, ears, fur. A later layer combines those into whole objects: "cat" or "dog."

This is why depth matters. A single layer can only combine the raw inputs in simple ways. With multiple layers, each one adds another level of abstraction, building up from simple features to complex concepts. The network learns what to compute at each layer during training. You don't have to tell it to look for edges or eyes. It figures that out on its own.

If three neurons in two layers can solve XOR, what happens when you keep adding more? Each neuron you add contributes another piece of the computation. Each layer lets the network combine results from the previous layer into something more complex.

How big do real networks get? GPT-3 has 96 layers, and frontier models today have even more. Even AI models that run on your smartphone have 20+ layers. The architecture is simple: the same building block repeated millions of times. The work is in finding the right weights.

## Training Deep Networks

So how do you train the weights in all these hidden layers? It turns out to be surprisingly direct. Every weight in the network, even ones buried deep inside, contributes to the final output. If it contributes to the output, it contributes to the error. That means every weight has a *gradient* (from the optimization chapter), telling you which direction to nudge it to reduce the error. So you can use gradient descent on every weight at once. The algorithm that makes this practical is called *backpropagation*. It computes the gradient for every weight by flowing the error signal backward through each layer.

<KeyInsight>
Stacking neurons in layers lets you compute anything. Each layer combines the previous layer's outputs into something more complex. Backpropagation trains all the weights at once by flowing the error signal backward through every layer. A network with ten thousand weights is no harder to train *in principle* than one with ten. It just takes more computation.
</KeyInsight>

## What's Next

A single neuron is a weighted sum and an activation function. Wire enough of them together, find the right weights, and you get intelligent behavior. Backpropagation makes finding those weights possible, even for networks with billions of them.

But what *is* that weighted sum, really? The next chapter introduces *vectors*, lists of numbers that describe things, and shows that a neuron's weighted sum is a geometric operation called a dot product. That connection explains why a single neuron can only draw a straight line through input space, why XOR is impossible for one neuron, and why activation functions are the key to making depth work.
