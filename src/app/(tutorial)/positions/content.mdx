# Where Am I?

<Lead>
The meaning of a sentence often depends on the *order* of the words — not just which words are there. But the attention system we built in the last chapter has no idea where any word is. Let's fix that.
</Lead>

## Word Order Matters

Think about "The dog bit the man" versus "The man bit the dog." Same words, completely different meaning — just from changing the order. Who's biting whom flips entirely.

This isn't a weird edge case. Word order is how language works. Moving a single word can flip a sentence's meaning entirely.

## Attention Is Position-Blind

Here's the problem: the attention system we built in the last chapter *can't tell any of these apart*.

Remember how attention works — each word gets an embedding (a list of numbers representing its meaning), and then we compute how much each word should pay attention to every other word using dot products. But the word "cat" gets the *same* embedding whether it's the first word or the last word. Nothing about position goes into the calculation.

That means "The dog chased the cat" and "The cat chased the dog" produce exactly the same attention pattern. The model literally cannot tell them apart. It's like reading a sentence where all the words are floating in a bag with no order — you can see which words are present, but you have no idea which came first.

We need to give the model a sense of position. There are two main approaches used in practice, and the difference between them turns out to be surprisingly important.

## The Simplest Fix: Distance Penalties

The most obvious idea: just subtract a penalty from the attention score based on how far apart two tokens are. If tokens are 3 positions apart, subtract 3 times some slope *m*. If they're 10 apart, subtract 10 × *m*:

> **score = (query · key) − m × distance**

This is called **ALiBi** (Attention with Linear Biases), and it's used by real models like BLOOM and MPT. It's simple and works: nearby tokens automatically get higher attention scores than distant ones.

<ALiBiToyTokensWidget>
Drag the slope around. At slope 0, both nouns get identical attention — the model is position-blind. Higher slopes make the closer noun win more decisively. Try the longer sentences to see the effect with bigger distance gaps.
</ALiBiToyTokensWidget>

Different attention heads get different slopes *m* — some heads have steep slopes and attend very locally (just the nearest few words), while others have gentle slopes and attend broadly across the whole context. This gives the model a range of distance sensitivities.

But ALiBi has a fundamental limitation: **the distance penalty is per-head, not per-query**. Every query in a given head gets the same distance falloff. The model can't decide "this particular word needs to look far back in the text" — the slope is baked into the head.

Consider: *"The president visited France. He said the treaty would be honored."* The pronoun "He" needs a steep distance falloff to latch onto the nearby "president." But "treaty" needs a gentle falloff to find its antecedent farther back. If both happen to land in the same attention head, they're stuck with the same slope — one of them gets the wrong distance curve.

More broadly, ALiBi's penalty is **content-blind**: it penalizes distance the same amount regardless of what the tokens actually say. And empirically, models using the approach we're about to see — RoPE — have consistently outperformed ALiBi models as scale and context length grow. Nearly every frontier model today (Llama, Mistral, Gemma, Qwen, DeepSeek) uses RoPE. Let's see why.

## Rotation Encodes Distance

There's a completely different way to measure how far apart two tokens are. It starts with a fact about dot products we haven't mentioned yet: **if you rotate two vectors by the same amount, their dot product doesn't change**. The dot product only cares about the angle *between* the vectors, not where they're pointing in absolute terms.

<RotationDotProductWidget>
Try dragging the base angle slider. Both vectors spin around the circle together, their x and y values change completely — but the dot product doesn't budge. Now change the angle between them and watch the dot product respond. It only cares about the gap.
</RotationDotProductWidget>

Now imagine giving each token a pointer on a circle. The token at position 0 isn't rotated at all. Position 1 is rotated a little. Position 2 is rotated twice as much, position 3 three times as much, and so on. Tokens close together get rotated by similar amounts, so their pointers still nearly align and their dot product is high. Tokens far apart get rotated by very different amounts, so their pointers end up pointing in different directions and their dot product is low. And the dot product depends only on the *gap*, not on the absolute positions.

<RotationPositionWidget>
Slide token A's position around while keeping the gap fixed — the x and y components change, but the dot product stays the same. Now change the gap and watch the dot product change.
</RotationPositionWidget>

How quickly we rotate per position is called the **speed**. A higher speed means the pointers spread apart faster, so even a small gap produces a big angle difference and a low dot product. A lower speed means tokens have to be farther apart before their dot product drops significantly. Try adjusting the speed slider in the playground above to see this tradeoff.

## Applying Rotation to a Dimension

How do we connect this geometric idea to actual attention? Take one dimension of our token vectors — say "nouniness" — and split it into two coordinates: noun-x and noun-y. Now rotate that 2D point by position × speed. The dot product between a rotated query and a rotated key automatically favors nearby tokens, just like the circle demo above.

<RotationToyTokensWidget>
Start with rotation speed at 0° — both nouns get identical attention, just like in the attention chapter. Now drag it up. The closer noun starts winning because its key is rotated to a more similar angle to "it"'s query. Try different sentences to see the effect with different distances. This achieves the same distance preference as ALiBi — but through geometry rather than an explicit penalty.
</RotationToyTokensWidget>

This is the core of **RoPE** (Rotary Position Embeddings): instead of *adding* a distance penalty after computing the dot product, we **rotate** each word's query and key vectors by an angle that depends on position. The dot product naturally encodes distance.

## Multiple Rotation Speeds

A single rotation speed has a problem: if the speed is 15° per position, then tokens 24 positions apart have rotated a full 360° and look as if they're right next to each other. A fast-spinning dimension wraps around and creates false matches at multiples of its period.

The fix: **use multiple dimension pairs rotating at different speeds**. A 128-dimensional vector becomes 64 pairs, each rotating at its own rate. The first pair rotates fast — maybe 30° per position — while later pairs rotate progressively slower, down to fractions of a degree per position.

Think of it like the hands on a clock: the second hand spins fast, the minute hand slowly, and the hour hand barely moves. A single hand wraps around every 60 seconds and can't distinguish 1:00 from 2:00. But the *combination* of all three hands is unique at every moment. Similarly, no two token positions share the same combination of rotation angles across all 64 dimension pairs. The slow-rotating pairs disambiguate positions that the fast-rotating pairs confuse.

This also gives each query **control over its own distance sensitivity**. A query vector distributes its signal across all 64 dimension pairs. If a query has large values in the *fast-rotating* pairs, those pairs' contributions decay rapidly with distance — making the query very distance-sensitive. If the query concentrates its signal in *slow-rotating* pairs, the dot product barely changes with distance — making the query attend broadly. Different tokens naturally produce different energy distributions, so each query gets its own distance curve — unlike ALiBi, where every query in a head is stuck with one slope.

## You Don't Need to Double the Dimensions

You might worry: splitting each dimension into x/y means we need *twice* as many dimensions. But in practice, models don't add new dimensions — they **pair up adjacent existing dimensions**. A 128-dimensional head vector becomes 64 pairs: dimensions 1 and 2 form the first pair, 3 and 4 the second, and so on. Each pair is treated as the x and y coordinates of a 2D point and rotated together.

This works because rotation preserves the *length* of each pair (√(x² + y²) stays the same) while changing its *direction*. No information is destroyed — it's just rearranged. The model learns during training how to organize information into pairs that work well with their assigned rotation speeds.

<KeyInsight>
The dot product of two vectors equals |A| · |B| · cos(θ). Content lives in the magnitudes |A| and |B| — how "noun-like" or "filler-like" a token is. Position lives in the angle θ between them. Rotation changes the angle without touching the magnitudes, so **content and position information stay cleanly separated.**
</KeyInsight>

## Does Rotation Tell Us Direction?

You might notice something: cos(θ) = cos(−θ). The dot product between two rotated vectors is the same whether word A is 3 positions *before* or 3 positions *after* word B. Rotation encodes *distance* but not *direction*.

This turns out to be fine. Language models already have a mechanism for direction: **causal masking**. During generation, each token can only attend to tokens that came *before* it — future positions are masked to −∞ before softmax, so they get zero attention weight. RoPE handles "how far apart," and causal masking handles "which direction."

## Scaling to Huge Context Windows

RoPE's base rotation speed is controlled by a parameter θ\_base, typically 10,000. This means the slowest-rotating dimension pair completes one full revolution over roughly 10,000 positions. Beyond that, positions start to become ambiguous — similar to how a 12-hour clock can't distinguish AM from PM.

To support longer contexts, techniques like **YaRN** and **NTK-aware scaling** stretch the rotation frequencies so that the slow pairs rotate even more slowly. It's like adding an even slower "day hand" to the clock — now you can tell apart any two times across an entire day, not just within 12 hours.

This isn't free: stretching frequencies spreads out the angular resolution, so the model needs fine-tuning to adapt. But it works remarkably well in practice. This is how Llama 3.1 handles 128K tokens and how Gemini extends to over a million.

## What We've Built

Position encoding solves attention's blind spot. We covered two approaches:

1. **ALiBi** — subtract a linear distance penalty from attention scores. Simple, but the penalty is per-head: every query in the head gets the same distance curve.
2. **RoPE** — rotate query and key vectors by position-dependent angles across pairs of dimensions. Each query naturally controls its own distance sensitivity through the distribution of signal across fast and slow pairs.

RoPE is used by virtually every frontier model today, and its elegant geometry — encoding position through rotation, with different dimension pairs at different frequencies — is why modern language models can handle context windows of over a million tokens.
